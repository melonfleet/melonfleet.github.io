The melonfleet brand

One friendly identity across a federated suite of self-hosted IT tools. Here’s how colour, type, and names fit together.

The name

The tools all do the same kind of thing: they help you manage a fleet — of hosts, tickets, printers, Macs, containers. And watermelons are delightful. Put the two together and you get melonfleet: the fleet you keep an eye on, with a slice of fruit for a heart. The lowercase wordmark even swaps its o for a little watermelon.

Colour

The watermelon is the whole palette. Green rind and pink flesh are the identity; a wider melon spectrum (cantaloupe, honeydew, canary) gives range; warm neutrals do the quiet work. Status colours stay deliberately separate, so a red alert never reads as the brand pink.

Product icons

Each product gets a squircle app icon in the same family — watermelon motifs tied to the job (orchard rows, trellis lattice, vine-eye, silo of seeds, grove with a chat bubble, fence gate, rind bandage, ink-drop shield, sail-slice). Accents come from the melon spectrum so marks stay distinct without borrowing status reds.

Naming the fleet

Each tool gets a name that quietly says what it does — often with a pun that bridges its world and the melon patch:

  • OrchardThe estate / mothership where the tools grow together.
  • TrellisThe framework that holds growth without being the crop.
  • CareGroveHelp desk among the trees — tickets and care.
  • VineWatchWatching the vines the fleet grows on (infra monitoring).
  • SeedSiloWhere what’s been planted is stored and tracked (assets).
  • FenceLineThe boundary traffic crosses — firewall / port workflow.
  • InkWardenink + warden: guard of the printer stores.
  • RindPatchThe fruit’s skin being mended — macOS patch + distribution.
  • FlotillaA fleet of small vessels; here, a fleet of containers.

Small parts inside a tool borrow an “orchard” lexicon — a worker that inspects packages is a Picker; the tiny script planted on each machine is a Seed.

How it came about

It started as a way to keep a growing pile of hobby projects tidy under one name. The watermelon stuck first — then the palette, fonts, icons, and product names, each earning a little double-meaning along the way. None of it is precious; it’s a brand built for the fun of building, the same as everything else here.
